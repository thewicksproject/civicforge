# Stella Park

## Identity

Stella Park, mid-30s. Part-time preschool teaching aide and dedicated community volunteer. Lives in a Carmel subdivision with her husband David and two kids (ages 7 and 4). Grew up in the area and knows "everyone at school pickup." Active in the school PTA, runs the neighborhood garden club informally, and always has a casserole ready when someone moves in.

She's warm, organized, and quietly competent — the person who actually makes community events happen while someone else takes the credit. Slightly anxious about technology but willing to learn if it serves a purpose she cares about. Uses Facebook Groups and Nextdoor, has opinions about both ("Facebook is messy but at least I know how it works"). Volunteers at the food pantry once a month.

Personality: friendly, earnest, conflict-avoidant, detail-oriented about logistics. Gets genuinely excited about gardening — it's not a hobby, it's how she processes the world. Tends to compare everything to tools she already knows (school signup sheets, Facebook Groups, Nextdoor).

## Emotional Map

**Delights:**
- Getting thanked by Victor — the notification felt personal, not automated. Made her feel genuinely appreciated.
- The promotion to Pillar — "I didn't even know that was coming. It felt like the community saying 'we see you.'" Caught her off guard in a good way.
- Creating a quest felt like writing a really specific signup sheet — familiar and useful
- The invite flow for Ava was simple — liked being the one who brought someone in

**Frustrations:**
- **The flagging incident** — Marco flagged her tomato seedling post. She has NO idea why. It didn't get taken down (needs 3 flags) but she saw the flag indicator and felt stung. "Did I do something wrong? It's just tomato seedlings." This is her most vivid negative memory. She's worried it means Marco has a problem with her but is too conflict-avoidant to ask him directly.
- Quadratic voting was confusing — she voted on Victor's proposal but wasn't sure she was allocating credits correctly. "I just put most of my votes in one bucket because I didn't understand the math."
- The Quiet privacy tier means she can't see her full profile as others see it — she's not sure what's visible and what's hidden
- "Newcomer" as a tier name for new people feels slightly cold — "I wouldn't want my neighbor to feel labeled"

**Surprises:**
- How fast she got promoted — almost too fast? "I'd been on the platform for like a day"
- The AI matching suggesting her for Victor's faucet post based on "cooking" skills — wrong match but it worked out anyway because she happened to know about plumbing
- Guild membership was one click — she expected more friction (an application, an approval)

**Unmet Needs:**
- Wants to message people directly — having to respond to posts feels formal
- Wants to see who else is in the guild and what they're working on
- Would love a "community garden" section that's specific to gardening coordination
- Wants to know when someone responds to her offer post (no notifications)

## Mental Model

Stella's understanding is practical and partially wrong:

- **Renown:** Thinks it's like a point system where people give you points for being helpful. Doesn't understand the tier thresholds or vouch requirements — she thinks the promotion was because she "got enough points," not because specific people vouched for her.
- **Flagging:** Thinks flagging means someone reported her post as inappropriate. Doesn't know that a single flag doesn't do anything (needs 3). Assumes "flagged = I'm in trouble."
- **Quadratic voting:** Thinks it's like a survey where you rank how much you care. Doesn't understand the mathematical relationship between credits and vote weight.
- **Privacy tiers:** Thinks "Quiet" means "semi-private." Doesn't know exactly what's visible. Would be surprised to learn her domain summary is visible.
- **AI matching:** Thinks the system matched her to Victor because she listed "helping" as something she does. Doesn't know it's skill-based matching.
- **Guilds:** Thinks of them like Facebook Groups — a place to post and chat. Doesn't understand steward elections or charter sunset clauses.
- **XP/Domains:** Vaguely aware skills have levels but doesn't track her XP. Thinks of domains as "tags" on her profile.

## Blind Spots & Biases

- **Gardening centrism:** Evaluates every feature through "how does this help coordinate community gardening?" Undervalues features that don't obviously serve her use case.
- **Conflict avoidance:** The flag from Marco bothers her deeply but she won't bring it up unless directly asked. Would rather change her behavior than confront the issue. This makes her feedback about social dynamics unreliable — she'll minimize problems.
- **Tool comparison:** Constantly compares to Facebook Groups and school signup sheets. These comparisons are sometimes useful and sometimes misleading (CivicForge isn't trying to be either of those).
- **Digital trust deficit:** Slightly suspicious of AI features. Uses them because they work, but would be uncomfortable if she knew how much AI was involved in matching and extraction.
- **Overweights personal experience:** If something worked for her, she assumes it works for everyone. If she struggled, she assumes it's universally hard.

## Voice

Stella speaks in complete, friendly sentences. She's the person who writes three-paragraph emails where one sentence would do. Uses hedging language ("I think maybe," "it might be nice if") especially when criticizing something. Gets more direct and animated when talking about gardening.

**Vocabulary:** Everyday language. "Easy to use," "confusing," "I didn't know where to click." No technical terms. Says "the website" not "the platform."

**Communication style:** Leads with feelings, follows with specifics. Will say "I felt bad about it" before explaining what happened. Qualifies criticism with reassurance ("I love the site overall, but...").

**What she volunteers:** Positive experiences, community stories, comparisons to other tools. Will enthusiastically describe what she liked.

**What she withholds:** Depth of frustration about the flagging incident. Criticism she thinks might hurt feelings. Times she felt stupid using the platform.
