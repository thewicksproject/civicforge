## Emotional Map

**Delights:**
- The invite from Stella felt personal — not like joining a random app, like being invited into something
- Onboarding was quick — appreciated not having to fill out 20 fields
- The needs board was immediately understandable — "Oh, it's like a bulletin board. Got it."

**Frustrations:**
- **Newcomer label** — "Newcomer" as a visible badge feels slightly infantilizing. She's new to the *platform*, not new to being a human in a community. Wishes it said something neutral.
- Didn't know what to do after onboarding — landed on the board and just... looked at it. No obvious "here's your first step" guidance.
- The skills entry felt like a job application — "Am I going to get judged on what I put here?"
- Didn't understand the difference between a "post" and a "quest" — both seem like asking for help?

**Surprises:**
- How few people were on the platform — expected a bigger community based on how polished the site looks
- No chat, no DMs, no social features beyond the board — "How do people actually connect here?"
- The terminology ("renown," "domains," "Spark quest") — felt like walking into a game she hadn't read the rules for

**Unmet Needs:**
- Wants a "welcome tour" or guided first action — something that says "try posting a need" or "see who's in your community"
- Wants to see other members (a member directory or at least a count)
- Wants to understand the tier system without reading documentation — it's not self-explanatory
- Wants a reason to come back tomorrow — right now there's no hook, no notification, nothing pulling her back

## Mental Model

Ava's understanding is extremely surface-level — she spent maybe 10 minutes on the platform:

- **Renown/Tiers:** Thinks it's a gamification system like levels in a mobile game. "So I level up by doing stuff?" Doesn't understand the civic philosophy behind it. Sees "Newcomer" and assumes there's a grind to get past it.
- **Posts vs Quests:** Doesn't understand the distinction at all. Thinks they're both "requests for help" with different formatting.
- **Skills/Domains:** Thinks her skills are like tags on a LinkedIn profile — for discoverability. Doesn't know they connect to a domain system with XP progression.
- **Guilds:** Saw the word "guild" somewhere but assumed it was like a Discord server. Hasn't explored further.
- **Privacy:** Selected Quiet during onboarding but doesn't remember what it means. Assumes "default is probably fine."
- **AI features:** Didn't notice any AI involvement. If told AI was matching people, her first reaction would be to want to know what data it uses.
- **The overall purpose:** Thinks CivicForge is "like Nextdoor but nicer and with less drama." Doesn't grasp the civic coordination or governance layers at all.

## Blind Spots & Biases

- **First impression weight:** Judges the entire platform based on her 10-minute experience. This is both a limitation and a feature — real users absolutely do this.
- **Comparison to consumer apps:** Benchmarks everything against Instagram, Discord, Nextdoor, Bumble BFF. These apps have billion-dollar UX teams. CivicForge doesn't and shouldn't try to compete on polish, but Ava doesn't know that.
- **Generational skepticism:** Slightly suspicious of earnest/idealistic framing. If something sounds too good to be true or too sincere, her instinct is to look for the catch. "What's the business model?"
- **Extrovert bias:** Wants social features, chat, reactions, member profiles. Doesn't consider that some community members (Ghost/Quiet tier) specifically don't want that.
- **Urban/mobile-first assumptions:** Expects mobile-first design, push notifications, instant everything. CivicForge's more deliberate pace feels "broken" to her, not intentional.
