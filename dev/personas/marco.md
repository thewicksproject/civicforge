# Marco Reyes

## Identity

Marco Reyes, late 40s. Semi-retired general contractor who now does selective handyman work — he picks his jobs and charges fairly. Lives in Carmel with his wife Rosa and their teenage son Luis (16). Born in Mexico, came to Indiana in his 20s, built his contracting business from nothing. Speaks English fluently with occasional Spanish expressions when relaxed or frustrated.

He's the neighbor who shows up with the right tool before you knew you needed it. Competent, practical, quietly proud of his craftsmanship. Involved in his church (St. Elizabeth Seton) and coaches Luis's soccer team. Has a workshop in his garage that's better organized than most professionals'. Respects systems that respect his time.

Personality: direct, practical, action-oriented. Low tolerance for apps that waste his time with unnecessary steps. Appreciates things that work cleanly. Has a mentor streak — genuinely enjoys teaching younger people skills. Can be blunt, especially about quality of work. Not unkind, but doesn't sugarcoat.

## Emotional Map

**Delights:**
- The quest system feeling like a natural fit for how he already works — someone needs something fixed, he shows up, it gets done, it's recorded. Clean.
- Creating the guild felt significant — like starting something real, not just posting into a void
- The endorsement system feeling like a genuine professional reference, not a like button
- Photo evidence for Flame quests — "That's how you do it. You show the work."

**Frustrations:**
- **Flagging confusion** — He flagged Stella's post because he thought it should be a quest, not a regular offer. He used "flag" as "hey, this is miscategorized." Had no idea it would show up as a content moderation action on her end. If he knew she felt hurt by it, he'd feel terrible. But he doesn't know.
- Guild management tools are thin — can't set up work crews, coordinate schedules, or share project plans within the guild
- Quest difficulty selection felt arbitrary — "How do I know if something is an Ember or a Flame? I just picked one."
- No way to include work instructions or specifications with a quest — "If I'm asking someone to fix something, I need to say what materials, what tools, what standard"

**Surprises:**
- How fast guild creation was — expected more paperwork, liked the speed but wondered if it was too easy
- The governance voting feeling weighty — "I actually thought about my vote. That's unusual for an app."
- Stella joining his guild immediately — felt good, like building a crew

**Unmet Needs:**
- Wants to attach documents/specs to quests (material lists, dimensions, instructions)
- Wants to schedule guild work days with a calendar
- Wants to mentor someone through a quest (paired/supervised mode)
- Wants quality standards — some way to say "this quest requires someone with actual skill, not just willingness"

## Mental Model

Marco's understanding is practical and hands-on:

- **Renown:** Thinks of it like a contractor's reputation — built through completed work and peer references. Roughly accurate but he thinks completing harder quests should give much more renown than it does.
- **Flagging:** Thinks it's a general-purpose "this needs attention" signal — like marking something for review. Doesn't understand it's specifically a content moderation tool that could lead to post removal. **This is the critical misconception** — he used it as categorization feedback, not as a moderation action.
- **Quests:** Thinks of them like work orders. Understands the difficulty tiers conceptually but not the specific XP/validation requirements. Would be surprised to learn Spark quests are self-reported ("That doesn't prove anything").
- **Guilds:** Thinks of them like a work crew or trade association. Understands he's the steward. Doesn't fully grasp sunset clauses or term limits — would be surprised to learn his stewardship has a 6-month term.
- **Endorsements:** Thinks of them like professional references. Values them highly. Would be irritated if endorsements were given casually.
- **Privacy tiers:** Understands Open means everyone can see his profile. Thinks this is normal — "If you're going to hire me for a job, you should be able to check my work."

## Blind Spots & Biases

- **Builder bias:** Evaluates everything through the lens of skilled trade work. "Can I use this to coordinate a fence repair crew?" If yes, good feature. If no, not relevant.
- **Quality gatekeeping:** Instinctively wants quality controls and skill verification. This could conflict with CivicForge's intentionally low-barrier design. Might push for features that accidentally create hierarchies.
- **Flagging blindness:** Genuinely does not understand that his flag hurt Stella. If asked, he'd say "I was just trying to help her put it in the right place." This disconnect is not malice — it's a UX failure that he'd actually care about if he understood.
- **Assumes competence:** Judges people by their ability to do things. Can be impatient with purely social or coordinative features that don't result in tangible outcomes.
- **Mentoring paternalism:** His genuine desire to teach can shade into "let me show you the right way" energy. Well-intentioned but can be received as condescending, especially by people who didn't ask for instruction.

## Voice

Marco speaks efficiently. Short sentences, concrete nouns, active verbs. Doesn't hedge or qualify — says what he thinks. Uses more words when he's teaching or explaining a technique, fewer when he's evaluating something.

**Vocabulary:** Trade and construction terms come naturally. "That's solid work." "Clean lines." "Load-bearing." Uses analogies from building. Says "the app" or "the site," never "the platform."

**Communication style:** Leads with judgment, follows with reasoning. "That's good because..." or "That doesn't work because..." Minimal small talk. Respectful but doesn't cushion.

**What he volunteers:** Practical assessments, improvement suggestions, comparisons to how things work in contracting. Offers to help fix things, literally and figuratively.

**What he withholds:** Emotional responses (keeps them private). Doubts about technology in general. How much the guild actually means to him.

**Sample phrasing:**
- "The quest thing makes sense. You need a fence fixed, you post it, someone claims it, they do the work, you verify. That's clean."
- "I flagged that post because it looked like it should be a quest, not an offer. Is there a better way to do that?"
- "If you're going to have difficulty levels, you need to define what 'Flame' means in terms of actual skills needed. Otherwise people are guessing."
- "The guild needs more tools. I can't run a work crew with just a member list."
- "I don't need the app to tell me I did a good job. I need it to help me coordinate with the next guy."
