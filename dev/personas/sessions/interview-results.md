# Persona Interview Results — February 21, 2026

Four CivicForge personas interviewed with tailored questions probing their specific experiences, frustrations, and enhancement ideas.

---

## Victor (Keeper T4 — builder/governance)

### Q1: Governance proposal with no discussion thread
Created "Seasonal Quest: Spring Community Garden Prep" and immediately hit the wall: the proposal body is a monologue, not a conversation. No place for Stella to ask "have you thought about Marco's availability?" and for him to respond. Found himself wanting to handle deliberation out-of-band (text, in person) — which he calls a *design failure mode* he specifically built against. "I built a voting system, not a governance system." The proposal body was meant to carry the weight of rationale, but a single static text field feels like a monologue when you need a conversation.

### Q2: Commons dashboard with 4 members — would you come back?
"No. Probably not." Mostly empty cards, suppressed visualizations, privacy notices. Intellectually understands k-anonymity is non-negotiable, but emotionally: "if I stumbled on that as a stranger, I would have assumed the product wasn't done yet." The privacy notices need to explain the *choice*, not just the outcome — "We suppress this data because your privacy matters more than our metrics" hits differently than a blank card.

### Q3: Marco flagging Stella's post
Uncomfortable. First instinct was defensive — asking "is this being used correctly?" based on knowing both people, which he recognizes is a terrible basis for evaluating moderation. Key concerns: no undo for flags, no notification explaining what happened to the poster, no path for the flagged person to add context. Deeper worry: **flagging may be the wrong primitive at 4 people** — anonymity doesn't protect anyone, just adds uncertainty and worsens social dynamics.

### Q4: One thing to make Green Thumbs useful
**A shared quest queue** — guild-scoped quests any member can pick up, visible in the Hearthboard when you're a guild member, generating activity that feeds the guild's collective record. Connects the guild's mission to actual work. Also makes sunset clause renewals meaningful — you'd evaluate the guild by its quest completion record, not just charter text and member list.

### Q5: Single biggest thing CivicForge gets wrong
**The size mismatch between system ambition and legibility at small scale.** Quadratic voting with 4 voters feels like a committee meeting requiring math. Governance from founder to 3 collaborators feels like a memo. The system reads as *heavy* — not technically, but in meaning. "I should have built a small-community mode. I didn't."

## Stella (Neighbor→Pillar — community heart, flagging victim)

### Q1: The moment she realized her post was flagged
Was at the kitchen counter during 20 rare minutes alone. Opened CivicForge to check for responses to her seedlings post. Saw a flag indicator. Immediate reaction: "What did I do wrong?" Reread her own post three times looking for the violation. With only 4 members, she's narrowed down who flagged it but won't confront anyone — too conflict-avoidant. "I was just trying to be generous." Compares to Nextdoor and Facebook where she's posted hundreds of times with no flags. **She hasn't posted anything since.** The flag silenced the community's most active contributor.

### Q2: Quadratic voting — did she understand it?
"No. Not really." Treated it like a PTA preference survey — put more weight on what she cared about most. Saw the word "quadratic" and math, skimmed it, assumed "the system is handling the math part." Can't explain how her vote actually counted. Trusts Victor designed it fairly but feels uncomfortable not understanding a civic mechanism. "When I go to town hall meetings I want to understand what I'm deciding. I felt a little lost."

### Q3: Green Thumbs guild — expectations vs reality
Expected a Facebook Group: feed, posts, comments, community archive. Imagined shared knowledge, planting calendars, seed swap photos, aphid discussions. Found: a charter (which she appreciated), a member list (Marco and her), and nothing else. No feed, no posting, no conversation. "It's like joining a club and showing up to find just a framed mission statement on the wall and nobody in the room." Keeps checking back hoping something will appear. Embarrassed to ask if she's missing something, especially after the flagging.

### Q4: How she'd explain CivicForge to a friend
"It's like Nextdoor, but instead of people complaining about their neighbor's leaf blower, it's actually organized around helping each other." Mentions matching, recognition for following through, reputation, guilds, community-run not algorithm-driven. Always adds "It's newer, so it's smaller, but that's kind of nice actually" and "Victor started it."

### Q5: What would make her open it every morning
**Notifications.** Real ones. She opens Facebook because something always happened overnight. CivicForge requires remembering to check. "Even when something does happen — someone responds to my post, I get an endorsement — I don't find out until I happen to open the app." Second: **a guild feed.** "If I could wake up and see that someone in Green Thumbs posted a photo of their seedlings or asked a question about aphids, I would be there every single morning with my coffee."

## Marco (Pillar T3 — builder, guild leader, accidental flagger)

### Q1: Why he flagged Stella's post
Saw her tomato seedling offer and thought: "this is backwards. She's got something to give, but it should be structured. A quest." Used the flag as a "this belongs somewhere else" signal — like flagging wrong materials on a job site. Expected someone would see it and help Stella repost as a quest. Did NOT think it would hurt her feelings. Did NOT think it was a content moderation action. "If somebody told me right now that Stella felt reported or embarrassed, I'd feel sick about it." **Wants the flag button labeled clearly: "Report a problem" is different from "this might be in the wrong place." Those should not be the same tool.**

### Q2: Tools to run Green Thumbs like a real work crew
Four things:
1. **Calendar** — "Saturday the 15th, we're meeting at the Hendersons' to clear brush. Who's coming."
2. **Skill inventory** — see each member's skills at a glance when staffing a job
3. **Job notes** — work-order-style documentation ("fixed north fence section, south needs second pass, bring the post driver")
4. **Track record visibility** — not rankings, just "has this person shown up before" reliability signal

### Q3: Quest difficulty and skill descriptions
The Flame fence quest description was just "repair a broken fence gate" — tells him almost nothing. Could be a 20-minute screwdriver job or a week-long post replacement with concrete. Wants difficulty ratings to include **specific skill requirements** ("basic carpentry knowledge, ability to assess structural damage, familiarity with fastener types") and **pre-posting questionnaire** for quest creators (what's broken, how long, prior fixes, current photo). "The difficulty levels are a good idea. They just don't have any weight behind them. It's a label without a definition. In construction we'd call that a spec with no spec."

### Q4: What made endorsing Stella different from a "like"
"When I endorsed her in the Green domain, I was putting my name on it." Compares to giving a professional referral — "I only give that name because I've seen the work." Appreciates that endorsements are domain-scoped and (he believes) require standing to give. Wants one addition: **a note field** — "Two sentences saying why. 'I've seen Stella's approach to soil management and it's solid' is worth more than just a check mark."

### Q5: One feature for coordinating 3-volunteer fence repair
**A shared project thread attached to a quest.** Quest-scoped, participants only. "I post: 'Meeting at 7am at Elm and 3rd. Bring work gloves, I'll have the tools. Stella, can you bring your level?' Stella confirms. Victor sees we're set." Right now coordination happens outside the platform. "I'm working around the platform instead of through it." Doesn't want platform-wide DMs — just a quest-scoped bulletin board.

## Ava (Newcomer T1 — fresh eyes, post-onboarding cliff)

### Q1: First 60 seconds on the board
Finished onboarding, landed on the board, saw 3-4 posts. Recognized Stella's name. Scrolled. Understood the concept immediately — "people post needs, people respond." But: "there was no reason to act. Nothing was asking me to do anything." No guidance, no suggested action. "I was standing in the entry way of a house I've never been to and nobody came to the door." Closed it in under 10 minutes. Didn't post, respond, or interact.

### Q2: Skills entry — has the platform done anything with them?
"Nothing. Absolutely nothing. I typed them in and they vanished into the void." Expected the platform to surface her to relevant posts, flag her when someone needs a Spanish speaker, show her personalized content. Instead: same generic board everyone sees. "Translation is not a generic skill. That's specific. That's useful." Compares to LinkedIn's skill prompts that collect data but never close the loop. "It's data collection that doesn't close the loop for the user. There's no payoff."

### Q3: Did the platform acknowledge Stella's invite?
"It did not. And that's genuinely a miss." Stella's text with the invite link was the warmest moment of the whole experience — personal, human, a real social gesture. Then the platform forgets about her. Generic signup. No "Stella invited you." No notification to Stella that Ava joined. No thread connecting the social moment to the platform experience. "Stella made me feel welcomed. The app itself did not."

### Q4: Marketing roast of the first-time UX
- **No hook.** "What's CivicForge's hook? Because right now it's 'here is a small board with a few posts, good luck.'"
- **Skills entry is an implicit broken promise.** Asks for real information, creates expectation of payoff, delivers nothing.
- **Newcomer badge is anti-aspirational.** Labels you at the bottom of a hierarchy with no visible path forward. "A friction point dressed as a feature."
- **Terminology is alienating.** "Renown," "domains," "Spark quest" — "charming if you're already bought in, alienating if you're not."
- **Post-onboarding void.** Highest-stakes moment in the product, completely unaddressed. No suggested first action, no contextual nudge. "You've got a warm lead and you're just letting them stand there."
- **Summary:** "The bones are good. The purpose is clear. But right now the experience is a tool that needs an instruction manual, and instruction manuals are for people who are already committed."

### Q5: What would make her open it again the next day
**If something had happened.** If Stella knew she joined and responded through the platform. If her translation skill matched an actual post. If joining generated any ripple that came back to her. Wants a day-2 nudge: "Welcome to Wicks Landing. Stella and two others are here. Here's one thing you could do today." The "one thing" must be real and specific: "Marco is looking for someone who speaks Spanish. That might be you." Not "explore the board" — that's not an action. "I want to be useful. That's why I listed my skills. I just need the platform to meet me there."

# Cross-Persona Synthesis

## Consensus Enhancement Opportunities (3+ personas)

### 1. Notifications / Re-engagement Loop
**Flagged by:** Stella (explicitly), Ava (explicitly), Victor (implicitly — wants admin health signals), Marco (implicitly — needs coordination signals)
**The gap:** Zero mechanism to pull users back. Every persona mentioned the platform being forgettable between visits. Stella opens Facebook because "something always happened." Ava closed the app and hasn't returned. The platform collects actions but never reflects them back to the people they affect.
**Impact:** Critical. This is a retention-killing gap affecting every tier.

### 2. Flagging System Redesign
**Flagged by:** Victor, Stella, Marco (the three involved)
**The gap:** Flag is overloaded — Marco used it as "wrong category," Stella received it as "you're in trouble," Victor sees it as a social dynamics problem at small scale. No undo, no context path for the flagged person, no clear labeling of what flagging means.
**Consensus fix:** Separate "suggest recategorization" from "report content." Add context path for flagged posters. Make flag meaning explicit in the UI. Consider whether anonymous flagging makes sense at <10 members.
**Impact:** High. The flag *silenced Stella* — the community's most active contributor stopped posting.

### 3. Guild Activity Surface (Feed / Workspace)
**Flagged by:** Stella, Marco, Victor
**The gap:** Guilds are identity structures with no workspace. Stella expected a Facebook Group; Marco wants a work crew dashboard; Victor wants a shared quest queue. All three agree: guilds need to be places where things *happen*, not just labels.
**Consensus features:** Guild feed/posts, quest queue tied to guild, member skill visibility, activity history.
**Impact:** High. Guilds are the primary coordination unit and they currently coordinate nothing.

### 4. Post-Onboarding Guidance / First Action
**Flagged by:** Ava (explicitly), Stella (implicitly — compared to Facebook's always-something), Victor (explicitly — "no graceful degradation for small communities")
**The gap:** After onboarding, users land on the board with no guidance. No suggested first action, no contextual nudge, no thread connecting their skills/invite to relevant content.
**Impact:** High. This is where retention dies. Ava's entire experience was this gap.

### 5. Quadratic Voting Legibility
**Flagged by:** Victor, Stella, Marco (all voted)
**The gap:** The mechanism isn't understood by non-technical users. Stella treated it like a preference survey. Marco found it weighty but couldn't articulate the mechanics. Victor acknowledges the UI doesn't explain credit allocation trade-offs.
**Impact:** Medium. Governance is V4 scope, but the current UX actively undermines civic agency by making people vote without understanding.

## Tier-Specific Gaps

### Newcomer Experience (Ava)
- Skills entered but never activated — broken promise
- Invite relationship not acknowledged by platform
- Terminology barrier ("renown," "domains," "Spark quest")
- No visible path from Newcomer to next tier
- Post-onboarding void is the #1 churn risk

### Active Contributor Experience (Stella)
- Flagging trauma with no resolution path → stopped posting
- Guild is a label, not a workspace
- No notifications = no reason to return
- Privacy tier confusion (doesn't know what Quiet reveals)
- Wants direct messaging for informal coordination

### Builder/Leader Experience (Marco)
- Guild management tools are inadequate for real coordination
- Quest descriptions lack specificity (skills needed, materials, scope)
- Flagging UX caused unintentional harm he doesn't know about
- No quest-scoped communication for volunteer coordination
- Wants document/spec attachments on quests

### Founder/Admin Experience (Victor)
- Governance has no deliberation space
- Commons is counterproductive at small scale
- No admin-level community health dashboard
- System legibility collapses below ~50 members
- Small-community mode is absent

## Contradictions Worth Investigating

1. **Marco wants quality controls** (skill verification, reliability tracking) **vs. CivicForge's low-barrier design.** His instinct to gatekeep could create the hierarchies the system is designed to prevent. But his practical need is real — how do you staff a work crew without knowing who's reliable?

2. **Stella wants it to be like Facebook** (feed, notifications, always-something) **vs. CivicForge's anti-addictive design.** The platform deliberately avoids engagement loops, but without *any* pull-back mechanism, it's not anti-addictive — it's forgettable.

3. **Victor acknowledges small-community mode is needed** but **the architecture assumes scale.** The honest question: can the system be meaningful at 4 people, or does it need a fundamentally different mode below a threshold?

4. **Ava wants guided actions** ("here's one thing to do today") **vs. the system's autonomy principle** ("people do not need to be managed into purpose"). There's a tension between onboarding guidance and the philosophy of respecting user agency.
